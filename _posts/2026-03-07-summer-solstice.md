Where better to spend the shortest night of the year than in a tent on top of a Munro?

That was my plan as I caught the train North at 6pm from Queen Street. It was one of those stuffy hot days in the centre. I was glad once the train was moving and some breeze came through the open windows. There was something so enticing about catching a train to the middle of nowhere straight after work. Being in Glasgow at 6pm, and in my tent on a mountain by 10!

I had a picnic dinner on the train and watched the world go by. You get a proper sense of Scotland on this journey: beginning with the outskirts of Glasgow, and slowly leaving civilization behind until you reach Loch Lomond, then Crianlarich, Tyndrum, Bridge of Orchy, Rannoch Moor -- it's properly epic.

I arrived at Corrour train station just after 9pm. It's literally in the middle of nowhere. No car access, there's a small station house and a youth hostel about a mile down a gravel road - that's it! Me and one other lady got off, watching the train disappear and realising you are totally on your own is some feeling. She wasn't very chatty, and headed off towards the hostel. Leaving me, my tent, and lots of space.

I made a bit of a mad dash up Beinn Na Lap to try to chase the sunset. As it turns out there's really no rush this time of year, it never really got properly dark. There was a beautiful purple and orange hue as the sun set and I pitched my tent just shy of the summit. It really felt like I was the only human for hundreds of miles.

I didn't get much sleep as it was a very blustery night. At one point I was suddenly very awake when I thought some sort of wild mountain beastie was trying to break into my tent, only to come to my senses and realise it was the wind.

I got out of the tent just before 4am to catch the earliest sunrise of the year. I wandered up to the summit cairn with my stove and made porridge as the world woke up around me. The views were incredible, with different colours coming and going as the light grew.

Set off pretty sharpish, with a big day ahead. The first few hours of the day involved getting myself over to the Grey Corries. I went down the way I came up Beinn Na Lap, and then past Loch Treig and over towards Stob Ban.

<figure>
  <img src="/assets/DSC_7726.JPG" alt="The Grey Corries ridge stretching out ahead">
  <figcaption>Grey Corries.</figcaption>
</figure>

It was a steep climb up to the first, and a fun scree ski down before getting up onto the ridge proper. From there I ticked off Stob Choire Claurigh, Stob Coire an Laoigh, Sgurr Choinnich Mor, Aonach Beag and Aonach Mor. Getting up to Aonach Beag is a little sketchy this way: there's no path and you have to find a way up a daunting looking gully. It was totally fine, but with the heavy pack and weary legs I definitely hesitated before deciding it was doable.

<figure>
  <img src="/assets/DSC_7754.JPG" alt="Looking along the CMD ridge towards Ben Nevis">
  <figcaption>Ben Nevis via CMD.</figcaption>
</figure>

This was my first outing with a full pack for quite some time, so my quads were definitely feeling the 8,000+ ft of elevation gain! I was also, stupidly, very low on water for pretty much the entire ridge. I decided to head down after Aonach Mor, rather than go onto Ben Nevis via CMD because of this.

<figure>
  <img src="/assets/DSC_7759.JPG" alt="The descent towards the river and camping spot near Steall Waterfall">
  <figcaption>The way down to my camp spot for the night.</figcaption>
</figure>

I camped down by the river below Steall Waterfall and gave my legs a nice ice bath in the water.

It was absolutely pelting it down in the morning, and the midges finally made an appearance! I walked the short (~5km) trail back towards the road and got the shuttle bus into Fort William so I could catch an earlier train. I was cold and damp, so I got some dry layers on and had a very cosy nap next to the radiator on the train: the perfect end to an adventure!

As someone who has done almost all of my Munros by car, this was really inspiring. I loved doing it by public transport, and linking the two train stations on foot was really fun. I'm already eyeing up more ways to have weekends like this!

I also really liked the fact that I had made this route myself: linking trains and distinct munro groups into one big adventure. This has given me a lot of confidence to do more of this: figuring out my own way to tick off the 282.
